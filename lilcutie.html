Chef's Academy

The Kitchen
Lab

Every great chef is part scientist, part mathematician, and part safety expert. Here's everything you need to know before you step into the kitchen! 🧑‍🍳

🔥

Fire & Heat Safety

Always turn pot handles inward so they don't stick out over the edge — a bump could send boiling soup flying! Keep a fire extinguisher nearby and never leave a hot stove unattended. If a pan catches fire, slide a lid over it — this cuts oxygen and kills the flame. Never use water on a grease fire!

🔪

Knife Safety

A sharp knife is actually safer than a dull one — dull knives slip and cause accidents. Always curl your fingers into a "claw" grip when chopping to protect your fingertips. Cut away from your body, keep knives clean and dry, and never put knives loose in a drawer. Store them on a magnetic strip or in a knife block.

🧼

Hand Washing Rules

Wash hands for at least 20 seconds with soap before cooking, after touching raw meat, after using the bathroom, and after touching your face. This removes bacteria like Salmonella and E. coli that cause food poisoning. Use warm water — cold water doesn't dissolve oils as effectively.

🌡️

Safe Food Temperatures

The "Danger Zone" for bacterial growth is between 4°C and 60°C (40°F–140°F). Bacteria double every 20 minutes in this range! Chicken must reach 74°C (165°F) internally. Ground beef needs 71°C (160°F). Never leave cooked food at room temperature for more than 2 hours.

🥩

Cross-Contamination

Raw meat, poultry, and seafood carry bacteria that can spread to ready-to-eat foods. Always use separate cutting boards — red for meat, green for vegetables is a common colour code. Wash surfaces between tasks. Never place cooked food back on a plate that held raw food.

⚡

Electrical Safety

Keep all electrical appliances away from water and sinks. Unplug mixers, blenders, and toasters when not in use. Never put metal utensils into a toaster — even when it's unplugged! Check cords for damage before using. Always pull the plug by gripping the plug itself, never the cord.

🧊

Thawing Food Safely

Never thaw frozen food on the counter at room temperature — this puts the outer layer in the Danger Zone while the inside is still frozen. Safe methods: overnight in the refrigerator, under cold running water, or in the microwave (then cook immediately). Frozen chicken left on a counter for 2 hours can have millions of bacteria on the surface.

🧤

Burns & First Aid

For minor burns, run cool (not cold) water over the area for at least 10 minutes. Never use butter or toothpaste — these trap heat and increase infection risk. Use dry oven mitts, not wet ones (steam transfers heat instantly through wet fabric). Keep a first aid kit accessible in every kitchen.

🫙

Food Storage Safety

Store raw meat on the bottom shelf of the fridge so juices can't drip onto other foods. Use airtight containers to prevent contamination and slow spoilage. Label leftovers with the date — most last 3–4 days in the fridge. When in doubt, throw it out! Smelling food isn't reliable — many dangerous bacteria are odourless.

🟤

The Maillard Reaction

This is the most important reaction in cooking! When proteins and sugars in food are heated above 140°C (285°F), they react together to create hundreds of new flavour compounds and that beautiful brown colour. It's why bread crusts taste better than the inside, why seared steak tastes amazing, and why roasted coffee smells incredible. It's named after French chemist Louis-Camille Maillard (1912).

🍬

Caramelisation

When sugar is heated above 160°C (320°F), its molecules break apart and recombine into over 100 new compounds with nutty, butterscotch, and complex flavours. Different sugars caramelise at different temperatures: fructose at 110°C, glucose at 160°C, and sucrose (table sugar) at 160–180°C. This is pure chemistry happening in your pan!

🥚

Protein Denaturation

Raw egg whites are clear because proteins are coiled up. When heated, those proteins unfold (denature) and get tangled together — turning white and solid. The same happens with meat. Acid also denatures proteins — this is why ceviche "cooks" raw fish in lime juice without any heat, as the citric acid unfolds the fish proteins just like heat would!

🍞

Yeast & Fermentation

Yeast are tiny living fungi that eat sugar and produce carbon dioxide gas and alcohol as waste products. In bread dough, those CO₂ bubbles get trapped in the gluten network, making dough rise. One gram of yeast contains about 25 billion cells! At temperatures above 60°C, yeast cells die — which is why you bake bread in a hot oven to set the structure.

🧈

Emulsification

Oil and water don't mix — oil molecules are non-polar and water molecules are polar, so they repel each other. An emulsifier (like lecithin in egg yolk) has one end that bonds to water and one end that bonds to oil, acting as a molecular bridge. This is how mayonnaise is made — egg yolk emulsifies oil and vinegar into a stable, creamy sauce.

💧

Boiling & Pressure

Water boils at 100°C at sea level — but only because of atmospheric pressure. At high altitudes (like in the mountains), air pressure is lower, so water boils at around 90°C. This means food takes longer to cook! A pressure cooker does the opposite — it increases pressure inside, raising water's boiling point to 120°C, cooking food up to 70% faster.

🧂

Osmosis in Cooking

When you salt vegetables, water moves out of the cells through osmosis — water flows from areas of low salt concentration (inside the cell) to high concentration (outside). This is why salted cucumbers shrink and release liquid. Chefs use this to draw moisture out before cooking, or to season food from the inside when brining meat in saltwater.

❄️

Freezing & Ice Crystals

When food freezes slowly, large ice crystals form that pierce cell walls, causing mushy texture when thawed (think frost-bitten strawberries). Freezing food fast creates tiny ice crystals that preserve cell structure. This is why professional blast freezers operate at -40°C — ice cream frozen this way stays creamy because of microscopic crystal size.

🌈

pH & Colour Changes

Red cabbage contains anthocyanins — natural pH indicators! In acidic conditions (low pH, like vinegar) it turns bright pink-red. In alkaline conditions (high pH, like baking soda) it turns blue-green. Adding lemon juice to cut avocado prevents browning because the acid slows oxidation reactions. Blanching green vegetables in boiling water then shocking in ice water keeps them vivid green by destroying enzymes that cause discolouration.

⚖️

Baker's Percentages

Professional bakers express every ingredient as a percentage of the total flour weight — this is called baker's math. If a recipe uses 1000g flour and 620g water, the hydration is 62%. This system makes scaling recipes up or down effortless. A baguette is ~65% hydration, ciabatta is ~80%, and a bagel is ~55%. Knowing the percentage tells you the dough's texture before you even mix it.

📏

Scaling Recipes

To scale a recipe, divide the desired yield by the original yield to get the conversion factor, then multiply every ingredient by that number. Original recipe makes 24 cookies, you want 60? Factor = 60 ÷ 24 = 2.5. Multiply every ingredient by 2.5. But be careful — spices, leavening agents, and salt don't always scale linearly. A doubled cake doesn't always need exactly double the baking powder!

🌡️

Temperature Conversions

Celsius to Fahrenheit: multiply by 9, divide by 5, add 32. Fahrenheit to Celsius: subtract 32, multiply by 5, divide by 9. Quick reference: 0°C = 32°F (freezing), 100°C = 212°F (boiling), 180°C = 356°F (baking), 230°C = 446°F (pizza). A useful trick: 16°C ≈ 61°F — both numbers just swapped! For gas mark ovens: Gas Mark 1 = 140°C, each mark adds roughly 14°C.

🥄

Volume Conversions

3 teaspoons = 1 tablespoon. 16 tablespoons = 1 cup. 2 cups = 1 pint. 2 pints = 1 quart. 4 quarts = 1 gallon. In metric: 1 teaspoon ≈ 5ml, 1 tablespoon ≈ 15ml, 1 cup ≈ 240ml. When precision matters (especially in baking), always weigh ingredients in grams — a "cup of flour" can vary by 30% depending on how tightly it's packed!

⏱️

Cooking Time Ratios

Roasting time for meat follows a ratio: roughly 20 minutes per 500g at 180°C, plus 20 minutes extra. A 2kg chicken = (4 × 20) + 20 = 100 minutes. Rice follows a 1:2 ratio — 1 cup rice to 2 cups water. Pasta needs 1 litre of water per 100g. Doubling a cake recipe means the baking time increases by only about 10–15%, not doubles, because heat penetrates from all sides.

💯

Food Cost Percentage

Restaurants calculate food cost percentage to stay profitable: (Cost of ingredients ÷ Selling price) × 100. If a pasta dish costs ₹80 in ingredients and sells for ₹320, the food cost is 25%. Professional kitchens aim for 28–35%. If your recipe calls for 500g of chicken at ₹200/kg, the ingredient cost is ₹100. This maths helps chefs price menus fairly and run a sustainable kitchen.

🔄

Yield Percentage

Not all of an ingredient is usable. A yield percentage tells you how much of a purchased ingredient is actually edible after trimming. Whole carrots have ~82% yield (the rest is peel and tops). A whole chicken has ~67% yield after removing bones. To find how much to buy: Required amount ÷ Yield % = Purchase amount. Need 500g of peeled carrots? Buy 500 ÷ 0.82 = 610g.

🧮

Ratio Cooking

Many classic recipes are just ratios memorised by chefs. Vinaigrette: 3 parts oil to 1 part acid. Béchamel sauce: 1:1:10 (equal butter and flour, 10x the milk by weight). Shortcrust pastry: 2 parts flour to 1 part fat. Pancake batter: 1:1:1 (equal flour, milk, eggs by weight). Once you know the ratio, you never need a recipe again — just scale to however much you need!

📊

Nutrition Maths

Calories come from three macronutrients: carbohydrates (4 calories per gram), protein (4 calories per gram), and fat (9 calories per gram). Alcohol adds 7 calories per gram. So 100g of olive oil = 900 calories, while 100g of chicken breast ≈ 165 calories. Reading nutrition labels: % Daily Value is based on a 2,000 calorie diet. 5% or less is considered low, 20% or more is considered high for a nutrient.
